The Rest of the 272 Timeline


The history of the reconciliation efforts by Georgetown University in response to their 1838 sale of 272 enslaved people, displayed chronologically.

June-November 1838

The 1838 Sale

In 1838, Georgetown University sells 272 enslaved people to various slave holders and plantations farther south.

Learn More
November 27, 1848

The Van de Velde Letters

Ten years after the sale, Jesuit priest James Van de Velde sends a pair of letters to Georgetown University, pleading for the University to fulfill their promise to support the religious instruction of the 272 enslaved people sold by Georgetown by contributing to the construction of a church in Louisiana. Both letters were ignored by the University.

1838-1989

161 years go by

Georgetown does nothing to acknowledge its history for over 150 years.

October 1, 1989

Black Georgetown Remembered

As part of its bicentennial celebration in 1988-89, Georgetown University honores its African American Community with this video. Through the recollections of the members of the Georgetown black community, the documentary pays tribute to a community that thrived amidst many injustices. It features the remembrances of the decades of the 1940s to the 1950s when the population of Georgetown changed markedly.

Watch Here
September 24, 2015

The Jesuit Plantation Project

History Professor Sharon M. Leon, Ph.D, creates the Jesuit Plantation Project. This project it sfocused on uncovering details about the lives and experiences of the enslaved involved in the 1838 sale, rather than on their Jesuit owners. This is the first step towards finding the descendants of the original 272.

September 24, 2015

Georgetown Working Group on Slavery, Memory, and Reconciliation

President DeGioia announces the Working Group on Slavery, Memory, and Reconciliation. led by Fr. David Collins, S.J., Ph.D., this group was tasked with providing advice and recommendations to the president on "how to best acknowledge and recognize Georgetown's historical relationship with the institution slavery."

November 13, 2015

Renaming Mulledy Hall and McSherry Hall

President DeGioia announces he will be accepting a reccomendation from the recently created Working Group to rename Mulley and McSherry Hall. The new names of these buildings would be Freedom Hall and Remeberance Hall, respectively.
